01 Introduction

Research question

How did book bans in U.S. public schools evolve between 2021 and 2023 — across states, the themes most often targeted, and the authors most affected — and what do these patterns reveal about the cultural and political forces shaping who gets to read what?

Significance

Since 2021, book challenges in U.S. public schools have reached historic highs, with over 15,000 incidents recorded between 2021 and 2023 alone. The books most frequently targeted are those that center the experiences of people of color, LGBTQ+ communities, and women — suggesting that book bans are not random, but reflect deeper cultural and political forces. School is where children first learn whose stories are considered worth telling. When books about certain communities are repeatedly removed, it sends a message about whose experiences belong in the classroom.

California offers a particularly instructive case: with only three recorded bans in this period, it stands in stark contrast to Florida’s 6,535 — demonstrating both the impact of protective legislation like AB 1078 and the uneven distribution of censorship across the country. But the absence of formal bans does not mean the absence of pressure. It means the pressure has gone underground — into informal decisions, self-censorship, and quiet removals that leave no trace in any dataset.

02 Theoretical Framework

Three lenses for reading the data

Lens I

Critical Race Theory

Books by and about people of color are disproportionately targeted — not by coincidence, but because of systemic patterns. CRT holds that racism is ordinary, not aberrational — embedded in everyday institutions rather than the work of a few bad actors. It asks us to look past individual challenges and read them as one structure at work.

Lens II

Data Feminism

Many challenged books deal with women’s bodies, gender identity, and LGBTQ+ experiences. Data Feminism reminds us that numbers are never neutral: what gets counted reflects existing power hierarchies, so we keep asking whose interests this data serves. Books at the intersection of race and gender face the most intense scrutiny of all.

Lens III

Science & Technology Studies

Our data is never complete. Even nearly 16,000 records capture only reported bans — books quietly removed leave no trace. Our visualizations show patterns, but the true scope of censorship is larger than what we can document.

03 Methods

Data & methods

Every figure on this page is generated from a single, openly published dataset, cleaned from the PEN America Index. We document our source, cleaning, tools, and limitations here so the analysis can be inspected, reused, and reproduced.

Data sources

  • Primary source PEN America, Index of School Book Bans — pen.org/book-bans

Data cleaning decisions & steps

  • Scope U.S. public-school book bans, 2021–2023 — 15,960 records across 43 states. The unit of analysis is a single reported ban.
  • Theme coding Each title was assigned a theme by an LLM classifier — recording a confidence level and a short reason — then reviewed and spot-checked by hand. Note “Other / Unclear” is the classifier’s residual bucket.
  • Author names Standardized author names so that challenge counts aggregate correctly across spelling and formatting variants.
  • Aggregation Grouped the cleaned records into counts by theme, year, author, and state — the figures behind every chart.

Tools & technologies

  • Data cleaning & analysis Python (pandas) for cleaning and aggregation, with Seaborn for exploratory plots; an LLM-based classifier assigned each title a theme (confidence level and reason), reviewed and spot-checked by hand.
  • Visualization Chart.js for the charts (themes, timeline, authors, state comparison) and D3.js for the U.S. states choropleth map.
  • Website HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — a static site with no build step, deployed on GitHub Pages.

Visualization 1

Most frequently banned themes

This visualization shows the most common themes found in banned books. While “Other / Unclear” makes up the largest category (32.0%), the most common clearly identified themes are Sexuality / Sex Education (22.6%), Violence / Trauma (14.7%), Race / Racism (11.5%), and LGBTQ+ / Gender (10.7%). Together, these categories account for nearly 60% of all banned titles. This shows that books discussing identity, inequality, and personal experiences are challenged more often than books on topics such as religion or politics.

Through Critical Race Theory

Critical Race Theory helps explain why these themes appear so often in book bans. Rather than seeing book challenges as isolated events, it encourages us to look at the larger patterns. As Delgado and Stefáncic argue:

“Racism is ordinary, not aberrational — ‘normal science,’ the usual way society does business, the common, everyday experience of most people of color in this country.”Delgado & Stefáncic, Critical Race Theory: An Introduction

These patterns suggest that larger cultural and political debates shape children’s reading. As the quote makes clear, racism is not always obvious or intentional; it can be built systematically into institutions and policies, including decisions about what students are allowed to read. When books about marginalized communities are removed, students have fewer opportunities to learn about different cultures, experiences, and perspectives. Book bans, then, do more than remove titles from shelves — they shape the way children come to understand society.

Visualization 2

Book bans over time

The number of book bans rose each year — from 2,532 in 2021 to 3,362 in 2022, before climbing sharply to 10,066 in 2023. While the increase between 2021 and 2022 was relatively small, the number of banned books nearly tripled between 2022 and 2023. This suggests that book banning became far more widespread over time, rather than remaining a series of isolated incidents. The rapid increase may reflect growing public attention, organized political movements, or changes in state policy. As more books are challenged, students may have less access to diverse perspectives and discussions — including those about racial identity, gender, and other social issues.

Through Science & Technology Studies

This trend can be explored further through Science and Technology Studies (STS), which emphasizes that datasets are never complete or fully objective. As Boyd Davis, Vane, and Kräutli argue:

“The end-user may be unaware of what has happened to data behind the scenes, reinforcing the question ‘Can I believe what I see?’ — or, in important cases, not see.”Boyd Davis, Vane & Kräutli, “Can I Believe What I See?”

This reminds us that the PEN America dataset represents only the book bans that were reported — meaning some titles may have been removed quietly, without ever being documented. As a result, the visualization may not capture the full extent of book censorship. STS encourages us to question not only the data placed in front of us, but also what may be missing: how the data was collected, and what has been left out.

Visualization 3

Most-challenged authors

The data shows that Ellen Hopkins and Sarah J. Maas were challenged far more often than any other author — with 791 and 657 bans, respectively. The remaining authors each received between 154 and 213 bans, leaving a large gap between the top two and the rest of the field.

Through Data Feminism

This pattern can be analyzed further through Data Feminism, which argues that data should never be treated as neutral, because it reflects existing systems of power. As Rezai explains:

“Letting the numbers speak for themselves is viewed as unethical and undemocratic.”Rezai, “Data Stories for/from All: Why Data Feminism is for Everyone.”

Rather than simply noting that Hopkins and Maas top the ranking, Data Feminism asks why their work is targeted so often. Many of their books address gender, sexuality, identity, trauma, and mental health — subjects that receive heightened scrutiny in schools. Read this way, the chart is not merely a ranking of authors by challenged works; it is evidence of the social and political power structures that decide which experiences are accepted in schools and which are restricted.

Visualization 4

Top states vs. the national average

This visualization shows that book bans vary enormously across states. Florida recorded the highest number (6,535), followed by Iowa (3,703), then Texas (1,964), Pennsylvania (664), and Wisconsin (480). Every one of these states sits far above the national average of about 371 bans per reporting state — 15,960 bans spread across 43 states — with Florida alone accounting for more than seventeen times that average. These differences suggest that book bans are not evenly distributed across the country. Local education policies, political priorities, and community decisions all shape how often books are challenged, which means a student’s access to books and perspectives can depend heavily on the state in which they attend school.

Through Data Feminism

This visualization can also be read through Data Feminism, and especially its principle of considering context. At a glance, the chart seems to make a clear argument: a few states tower over everyone else, with Florida and Iowa dwarfing every other bar. But Data Feminism reminds us that numbers are never neutral. As Rezai puts it:

“Numbers, or raw data, are alone not enough to show results in works of research that have to deal with humans.”Rezai, “Data Stories for/from All.”

In other words, the chart cannot be fully understood through the height of each bar alone. It uses total counts rather than rates: without adjusting for population, the number of schools, district size, reporting practices, or how a “book ban” is even defined, the visualization can make some states look naturally more extreme while hiding the systems that produced the data. The national average is itself plotted as if it were just another state, which can make the comparison feel simpler than it really is. From a Data Feminist perspective, a stronger visualization would explain the dataset, supply context, and perhaps compare bans per school, district, or student. The issue is not that the chart is useless — it is that it needs framing, so viewers do not treat raw numbers as a self-evident truth.

Geographic distribution

Where bans were reported

Mapped across the country, the bans cluster sharply. A handful of states — Florida, Iowa, and Texas above all — account for the overwhelming majority of the record, while most states report only a few. California is the instructive outlier: one of the most populous states in the nation, it recorded just three bans — a direct reflection of protective legislation such as AB 1078.

Through Science & Technology Studies

But an empty space on the map is not the same as an absence of pressure. The PEN America Index captures only the bans that were formally reported; quiet removals and informal challenges leave no trace here. The geography we can see is shaped as much by what each state chooses to record as by what actually happens on its shelves.

05 Ethical Considerations

What we can — and cannot — claim

  • Data ethics. This project is not value-neutral. We draw on Critical Race Theory, Data Feminism, and Science & Technology Studies because these frameworks help us understand book bans as part of broader systems of racial, gender, and social inequality. As D’Ignazio and Klein argue in Data Feminism, data practices are never neutral — they reflect the priorities and power structures of those who collect and interpret them. We acknowledge that different theoretical perspectives could lead to different interpretations of the same dataset.
  • Limitations. Our analysis covers reported book-ban incidents between 2021 and 2023, rather than through 2025 as originally planned, because more recent data were not available in a complete and usable format. Many removals also occur informally or without public documentation, so the PEN America Index captures only reported incidents and likely underestimates the true scope of censorship. As Boyd Davis, Vane, and Kräutli demonstrate, visualizations can create an impression of completeness that the underlying data does not support — making it essential to be transparent about what our dataset cannot show. Our themes are, in addition, AI-assigned: the large “Other / Unclear” bucket is a reminder that automated classification is imperfect and that some titles resist easy labelling.
  • Stakeholder impact. Students are the primary stakeholders affected by book bans — particularly those from historically marginalized communities, including students of color and LGBTQ+ students — yet their perspectives are not directly represented in this dataset. Their experiences appear only indirectly, through the absence of books that might have represented their identities. As Delgado and Stefáncic remind us, racism is embedded in systems and institutions that appear neutral, and a dataset that cannot see the people most harmed by those systems risks reinforcing their invisibility. This silence is itself a form of erasure: when the people most harmed by book bans cannot be seen in the data, it becomes easier to dismiss their experiences as marginal or exceptional.

06 Annotated Bibliography

Sources & comparisons

MLA. Select any entry to read its annotation.

Two DH projects for comparison

Slave Voyages is a large-scale digital humanities database documenting over 36,000 slave trade voyages. It offers interactive maps and data visualizations that allow users to explore the scale and geography of the transatlantic slave trade. We use it as a comparative case study because it illustrates the ethical complexity of transforming historical atrocity into structured data.

Slave Voyages helped us think critically about how data visualizations can appear more complete than the underlying data actually supports. Like our project, Voyages makes large-scale patterns visible — but risks obscuring whose voices are absent from the archive. This comparison directly informed our Ethical Considerations section, where we acknowledge that our PEN America dataset only captures reported incidents. It also shaped our decision to design visualizations that make gaps visible rather than presenting our findings as a definitive picture.

This digital humanities project analyzes 1,651 banned books through computational methods, examining cover design, character representation, and visual patterns. The researchers found that 81% of covers feature at least one human figure, and 64% of those figures appear to be of nonwhite or ambiguous race. It is directly relevant to our project because it uses data visualization to reveal systemic patterns in book banning — the same approach we take with the PEN America dataset.

This project reinforced our understanding that banned books disproportionately feature characters of color and LGBTQ+ identities, supporting our application of Critical Race Theory and Data Feminism. It also demonstrated how combining quantitative analysis with interpretive narrative produces more meaningful insights than data alone. Their methodology — pairing visualizations with critical framing — directly informed how we designed our Analysis section. It showed us that asking “what does the data show” is always inseparable from asking “whose stories are being erased.”

Three readings

This foundational text introduces Critical Race Theory, arguing that racism is embedded in systems and structures that produce racially unequal outcomes even when they appear neutral. The authors trace the development of CRT from legal scholarship into broader applications across education and social policy. It provides our primary theoretical lens for interpreting patterns in our dataset.

CRT directly shaped how we interpret the disproportionate targeting of books by and about people of color in U.S. public schools. It helped us move beyond describing individual incidents and toward identifying the systemic patterns they reflect. This framework is visible throughout our Theoretical Framework and Analysis sections. It also informed our research question, pushing us to ask not just what is happening, but what these patterns reveal about cultural and political forces.

This article, published in Digital Humanities Quarterly, reviews D’Ignazio and Klein’s Data Feminism through the lens of intersectional feminist theory. It explains how data practices reflect existing power structures and argues that asking “data science for whom?” is essential to any ethical data project. It is relevant to our project because it connects feminist theory directly to digital humanities practice.

This review reinforced our understanding of why Data Feminism’s seven principles — particularly examining power and making labor visible — are essential frameworks for analyzing book bans. It helped us articulate why quietly removed books, those that leave no trace in our dataset, represent a form of invisible labor that our project must acknowledge. The article’s emphasis on asking “whose interests does this data serve?” directly shaped our Ethical Considerations section. It also strengthened our argument that book banning is not just a political issue but a data justice issue.

This article examines how data visualizations in humanistic research can create impressions of completeness and objectivity that the underlying data does not fully support. Drawing on their own experience with museum and archival collections, the authors identify three key sources of mistrust in cultural data visualization: omission and bias, naming and classification, and uncertainty and precision. It is directly relevant to our project because our dataset — like the cultural collections the authors analyze — captures only what has been formally reported, leaving a significant portion of real-world censorship invisible.

This reading directly informed both our Methods and Ethical Considerations sections. It helped us articulate why the PEN America dataset should be treated as a partial record rather than a complete census of book bans. The authors’ argument that visualizations can make incomplete data appear authoritative shaped our decision to be explicit about our dataset’s limitations throughout the project. Their framework also reinforced our commitment to pairing every visualization with a critical narrative — ensuring that our charts invite questioning rather than false confidence.

Data source

PEN America is a nonprofit organization that advocates for freedom of expression. Since 2021, it has maintained a publicly accessible index documenting book bans and challenges in public schools across the United States, organized by title, author, school district, state, and academic year. It is the primary dataset for our entire project.

We use this dataset to examine book bans in U.S. public schools between 2021 and 2023. After cleaning the index and assigning each title a theme via an AI classifier, it provides the foundation for all of our visualizations — tracking patterns in geographic distribution, targeted themes, and affected authors. Because PEN America only records reported incidents, this dataset also directly shaped our Ethical Considerations section — particularly our discussion of data limitations and archival silence. Every analytical claim in our project traces back to this source.
