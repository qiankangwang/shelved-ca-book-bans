01 Introduction

Research question

How did book bans in U.S. public schools evolve between 2021 and 2023 — across states, the themes most often targeted, and the authors most affected — and what do these patterns reveal about the cultural and political forces shaping who gets to read what?

Significance

Between 2021 and 2023, book challenges in U.S. public schools reached historic highs — this dataset alone records nearly 16,000 bans across 43 states. Schools are where children first learn whose stories are worth telling. When books about certain communities are repeatedly removed, it sends a message about whose experiences belong in the classroom.

02 Theoretical Framework

Three lenses for reading the data

Lens I

Critical Race Theory

Books by and about people of color are disproportionately targeted — not by coincidence, but because of systemic patterns. CRT asks us to look beyond individual incidents and recognize the broader structure they reflect.

Lens II

Data Feminism

Many challenged books deal with women’s bodies, gender identity, and LGBTQ+ experiences. Data Feminism helps us see how data practices reproduce existing power structures. Books at the intersection of race and gender face the most intense scrutiny of all.

Lens III

Science & Technology Studies

Our data is never complete. Even nearly 16,000 records capture only reported bans — books quietly removed leave no trace. Our visualizations show patterns, but the true scope of censorship is larger than what we can document.

03 Methods

Data & methods

Every figure on this page is generated from a single, openly published dataset, cleaned from the PEN America Index. We document our source, cleaning, tools, and limitations here so the analysis can be inspected, reused, and reproduced.

Data sources

  • Primary source PEN America, Index of School Book Bans — pen.org/book-bans

Data cleaning decisions & steps

  • Scope U.S. public-school book bans, 2021–2023 — 15,960 records across 43 states. The unit of analysis is a single reported ban.
  • Theme coding Each title was assigned a theme by an AI classifier, with a confidence level and a short reason. Note “Other / Unclear” is the classifier’s residual bucket.

Tools & technologies

  • Data cleaning Python / spreadsheet, plus an LLM theme classifier
  • Website HTML, CSS, JavaScript · Chart.js · D3.js · deployed on GitHub Pages

05 Ethical Considerations

What we can — and cannot — claim

  • Data ethics. This project is not neutral. We apply CRT, Data Feminism, and STS because we understand book bans as connected to broader systems of racial and gender inequality. Other frameworks would produce different interpretations of the same data.
  • Limitations. Our data only captures reported bans. Many removals happen quietly, without formal complaint. Our themes are also AI-assigned — the large “Other / Unclear” bucket is a reminder that automated classification is imperfect and that some titles resist easy labelling.
  • Stakeholder impact. The students most affected — particularly students of color and LGBTQ+ students — have no direct voice in this data. Their experiences appear only through the absence of books that might have represented them.

06 Annotated Bibliography

Two DH projects for comparison

Slave Voyages is a large-scale digital humanities database documenting over 36,000 slave trade voyages. It offers interactive maps and data visualizations that allow users to explore the scale and geography of the transatlantic slave trade. We use it as a comparative case study because it illustrates the ethical complexity of transforming historical atrocity into structured data.

Slave Voyages helped us think critically about how data visualizations can appear more complete than the underlying data actually supports. Like our project, Voyages makes large-scale patterns visible — but risks obscuring whose voices are absent from the archive. This comparison directly informed our Ethical Considerations section, where we acknowledge that our PEN America dataset only captures reported incidents. It also shaped our decision to design visualizations that make gaps visible rather than presenting our findings as a definitive picture.

This digital humanities project analyzes 1,651 banned books through computational methods, examining cover design, character representation, and visual patterns. The researchers found that 81% of covers feature at least one human figure, and 64% of those figures appear to be of nonwhite or ambiguous race. It is directly relevant to our project because it uses data visualization to reveal systemic patterns in book banning — the same approach we take with the PEN America dataset.

This project reinforced our understanding that banned books disproportionately feature characters of color and LGBTQ+ identities, supporting our application of Critical Race Theory and Data Feminism. It also demonstrated how combining quantitative analysis with interpretive narrative produces more meaningful insights than data alone. Their methodology — pairing visualizations with critical framing — directly informed how we designed our Analysis section. It showed us that asking “what does the data show” is always inseparable from asking “whose stories are being erased.”

Two readings

This foundational text introduces Critical Race Theory, arguing that racism is embedded in systems and structures that produce racially unequal outcomes even when they appear neutral. The authors trace the development of CRT from legal scholarship into broader applications across education and social policy. It provides our primary theoretical lens for interpreting patterns in our dataset.

CRT directly shaped how we interpret the disproportionate targeting of books by and about people of color in U.S. public schools. It helped us move beyond describing individual incidents and toward identifying the systemic patterns they reflect. This framework is visible throughout our Theoretical Framework and Analysis sections. It also informed our research question, pushing us to ask not just what is happening, but what these patterns reveal about cultural and political forces.

This article, published in Digital Humanities Quarterly, reviews D’Ignazio and Klein’s Data Feminism through the lens of intersectional feminist theory. It explains how data practices reflect existing power structures and argues that asking “data science for whom?” is essential to any ethical data project. It is relevant to our project because it connects feminist theory directly to digital humanities practice.

This review reinforced our understanding of why Data Feminism’s seven principles — particularly examining power and making labor visible — are essential frameworks for analyzing book bans. It helped us articulate why quietly removed books, those that leave no trace in our dataset, represent a form of invisible labor that our project must acknowledge. The article’s emphasis on asking “whose interests does this data serve?” directly shaped our Ethical Considerations section. It also strengthened our argument that book banning is not just a political issue but a data justice issue.

Sources for data

PEN America is a nonprofit organization that advocates for freedom of expression. Since 2021, it has maintained a publicly accessible index documenting book bans and challenges in public schools across the United States, organized by title, author, school district, state, and academic year. It is the primary dataset for our entire project.

We use this dataset to examine book bans in U.S. public schools between 2021 and 2023. After cleaning the index and assigning each title a theme via an AI classifier, it provides the foundation for all of our visualizations — tracking patterns in geographic distribution, targeted themes, and affected authors. Because PEN America only records reported incidents, this dataset also directly shaped our Ethical Considerations section — particularly our discussion of data limitations and archival silence. Every analytical claim in our project traces back to this source.
