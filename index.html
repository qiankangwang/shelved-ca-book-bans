01 Research Question & Significance

Research Question & Significance

How have book bans in California public elementary schools evolved between 2021 and 2025 in terms of geographic distribution, targeted themes, and affected authors — and what do these patterns reveal about the cultural and political forces shaping childhood reading in the state?

Elementary school is where children first learn whose stories are considered worth telling.

When books about certain communities are repeatedly challenged, it sends a message — not just to those communities, but to all children — about whose experiences belong in the classroom. Understanding these patterns is the first step toward protecting every child’s right to see themselves in the books they read.

02 Theoretical Framework

Three lenses for reading the data

This project draws on three theoretical frameworks to analyze book ban patterns in California elementary schools.

Lens I

Critical Race Theory

CRT helps us see that the books being challenged are not random. Books by and about people of color are disproportionately targeted — not because of coincidence, but because of systemic patterns that persist even within a legally protected environment. CRT asks us to look beyond individual incidents and recognize the broader structure they reflect.

Lens II

Feminist Theory

Feminist Theory extends this analysis to gender. Many of the most frequently challenged books deal with women’s bodies, gender identity, and LGBTQ+ experiences. Kimberlé Crenshaw’s concept of intersectionality is particularly useful here: books that sit at the intersection of race and gender — featuring characters who are both people of color and LGBTQ+ — face the most intense scrutiny of all.

Lens III

Science & Technology Studies

STS reminds us that our data is never complete. The PEN America dataset only captures reported challenges. Books quietly removed from shelves, or never purchased in the first place, leave no trace. Our visualizations show patterns — but the true scope of book censorship in California is likely larger than what we can document.

03 Ethical Considerations

What we can — and cannot — claim

This project has real limitations that we want to be transparent about.

  • Our data only captures what was reported. Many book removals happen quietly — a title disappears from a shelf without a formal complaint, or a librarian self-censors under community pressure. The PEN America index documents the visible surface of book censorship in California, but the full picture is likely larger than what our visualizations can show.
  • The students most affected have no direct voice in this data. Particularly students of color and LGBTQ+ students — their experiences appear only indirectly, through the absence of books that might have represented them.
  • This project is not neutral. Our decision to apply Critical Race Theory and Feminist Theory reflects a particular perspective — one that understands book bans as connected to broader systems of racial and gender inequality. We believe this framework reveals patterns that a purely descriptive analysis would miss. Readers should know that other frameworks would produce different interpretations of the same data.

04 Analysis Narrative

Overall trends, 2021–2025

The data reveals that book challenges in California elementary schools, while significantly lower than in states like Florida and Texas, have not disappeared in the wake of progressive state legislation. Between 2021 and 2025, California recorded [X] book challenges across [X] school districts — a pattern that demands closer examination precisely because it occurs within a legal framework explicitly designed to prevent it. The passage of AB 1078 in 2023 appears to have reduced formal challenges in subsequent years, yet incidents have continued, suggesting that legislation alone cannot fully resolve the cultural tensions driving book bans.

This is where Critical Race Theory offers a crucial insight. CRT reminds us that systemic bias does not disappear simply because a law prohibits it — it adapts. When formal book removal becomes legally risky, the pressure may shift to informal channels: a librarian quietly relocating a book to a less accessible shelf, a teacher choosing not to recommend a title, or a school administrator declining to purchase certain books in the first place. These informal acts of censorship leave no trace in our data, which means the numbers we present here likely undercount the true scope of the problem. The trend line in our visualization shows reported challenges — but the more significant trend may be the one we cannot see.

Geographic distribution

Geographically, book challenges in California elementary schools are not evenly distributed. Our data shows a concentration of incidents in [X regions/counties], particularly in areas with more politically conservative local governments and school boards, such as parts of Southern California including Orange County and the Inland Empire. By contrast, urban districts in the Bay Area and Los Angeles recorded comparatively fewer formal challenges — though this does not necessarily mean fewer books were quietly removed.

This geographic pattern invites a question that Science and Technology Studies is particularly well-equipped to ask: what does the map show, and what does it hide? Our visualization displays the locations of reported incidents, but it cannot capture the self-censorship that occurs when librarians and teachers in any district — conservative or progressive — anticipate community pressure and adjust their collections accordingly. As Boyd Davis, Vane, and Kräutli argue, a visualization creates an impression of completeness that the underlying data may not support. The blank spaces on our map do not necessarily represent districts where all books are freely available — they may simply represent districts where removals go unreported. Reading the map critically means paying as much attention to what is absent as to what is present.

Themes under attack

The thematic breakdown of challenged books in California elementary schools reveals a pattern that is difficult to attribute to coincidence. Books addressing LGBTQ+ identities, racial history, and gender representation account for a disproportionate share of all challenges. [X%] of challenged titles involve LGBTQ+ content, while [X%] address race or ethnicity. Books dealing with both themes simultaneously — stories featuring characters who are both people of color and LGBTQ+ — appear with particular frequency.

Critical Race Theory helps us name what this pattern represents. The books being challenged are not random selections — they are books that center communities that have historically been marginalized in American public life. When these books are removed from elementary school libraries, it is not simply a matter of taste or appropriateness. It is a decision about whose experiences are considered suitable for children to encounter, and whose are not. Feminist Theory adds another layer: many of the challenged titles involve women’s bodies, reproductive health, or non-binary gender identities, suggesting that anxieties about gender and sexuality are deeply intertwined with anxieties about race. The concept of intersectionality, developed by Kimberlé Crenshaw, is particularly illuminating here — the books that face the most intense scrutiny are often those that sit at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities, facing compounded pressure precisely because they refuse to tell only one kind of story.

Affected authors

The authors whose books are most frequently challenged in California elementary schools reflect the same pattern as the themes. Authors of color and LGBTQ+ authors — and particularly those who are both — are disproportionately represented among challenged titles. This is not simply about the content of the books; it is also about who is considered a legitimate voice in children’s education.

This raises a question that goes beyond data: what message does it send to a child of color, or a child who is questioning their gender identity, when the books that reflect their experience are the ones being removed? The absence of these books from library shelves is not a neutral act. It is a statement about whose stories belong in school — and whose do not. Our data can document the pattern, but it cannot fully capture the human cost of these removals for the students who might have found themselves, or found comfort, in the pages of a book that is no longer there.

05 Critical Conclusion

What the data tells us — and what it does not

Taken together, our data tells a story about the limits of legal protection. California’s AB 1078 and AB 1825 represent genuine commitments to educational equity and intellectual freedom — and they have made a difference. California’s challenge numbers remain far below those of states without similar protections. But the persistence of challenges within a legally protected environment reveals something important: book bans are not primarily a legal problem. They are a cultural and political one.

The forces driving book challenges in California elementary schools — anxieties about race, gender, sexuality, and who belongs in the American story — do not disappear when a law is passed. They adapt, finding new channels and quieter methods. Our data captures only the visible surface of this phenomenon. The deeper pattern is one that CRT, Feminist Theory, and STS help us see precisely because they ask us to look beyond what the numbers show, and to consider the systems, silences, and power dynamics that shape what gets counted in the first place.

The question is not whether book bans happen in California — they do. The question is what we choose to do with that knowledge.

Data can make patterns visible. But turning visibility into change requires the kind of critical, informed engagement that this project hopes to support.

06 Annotated Bibliography

Sources & comparisons

Data Source

PEN America is a nonprofit organization that advocates for freedom of expression. Since 2021, it has maintained a publicly accessible index documenting book bans and challenges in public schools across the United States, organized by title, author, school district, state, and academic year. This dataset is the primary source for our project’s analysis. We use it to filter and examine book challenge incidents in California elementary schools between 2021 and 2025, tracking patterns in geographic distribution, targeted themes, and affected authors. Because PEN America only records reported incidents, our analysis acknowledges that the dataset captures a partial picture — a limitation we address directly in our Ethical Considerations section using the framework provided by Boyd Davis, Vane, and Kräutli.

Theoretical Readings

This foundational text introduces Critical Race Theory, arguing that racism is not primarily a matter of individual prejudice but is embedded in legal systems, institutions, and social structures that produce racially unequal outcomes even when they appear neutral. The authors trace the development of CRT from legal scholarship into broader applications across education and social policy. In our project, CRT provides the primary theoretical lens for interpreting patterns in our dataset. When books by and about people of color are disproportionately challenged in California elementary schools — even within a state that has legally prohibited such bans — CRT helps us understand this not as a series of isolated incidents but as evidence of systemic bias operating below the level of formal policy.
This article examines how data visualizations in humanistic research can create impressions of completeness and objectivity that the underlying data does not fully support. Drawing on their own experience with museum and archival collections, the authors identify three key sources of distrust in cultural data visualization: omission and bias, naming and classification, and uncertainty and precision. This reading directly informs both our methods and our ethical framework. Because our dataset only captures reported book challenges, our visualizations risk suggesting a more complete picture than the data warrants. Following Boyd Davis, Vane, and Kräutli’s recommendations, we have designed our project to make its gaps visible — acknowledging what the data cannot show rather than presenting our findings as a definitive account of book banning in California.
This book applies intersectional feminist theory to data science and data visualization, arguing that data practices reflect and reproduce existing power structures unless actively designed to challenge them. The authors introduce concepts such as the “matrix of domination” and “invisible labor” to show how data collection, classification, and presentation embed assumptions about whose knowledge counts and whose experiences are made visible. In our project, Data Feminism provides the feminist theoretical framework for analyzing why books about women, gender identity, and LGBTQ+ experiences are disproportionately targeted. It also informs our design choices — particularly our commitment to transparency about data limitations and our effort to center the experiences of the communities most affected by book bans, rather than treating them purely as objects of analysis.

DH Projects for Comparison

Voyages is a large-scale digital humanities database documenting over 36,000 slave trade voyages between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries, hosted at Rice University. It offers interactive maps, timelines, and data visualizations that allow users to explore the scale and geography of the transatlantic slave trade. We use Voyages as a comparative case study in our theoretical framework because it illustrates both the power and the ethical complexity of transforming historical atrocity into structured data. Like our project, Voyages makes large-scale patterns visible through visualization — but as our analysis in the theoretical framework section shows, it also risks creating an impression of completeness that obscures whose voices are absent from the archive. This comparison helps us reflect critically on our own data practices and design decisions.
Voyant Tools is a free, open-source, web-based text analysis platform that allows users to upload or link any corpus of text and generate visualizations including word clouds, frequency trends, and keyword-in-context displays. It is widely used in digital humanities pedagogy and research as an accessible entry point to computational text analysis. We reference Voyant Tools as a comparison point for thinking about the affordances and limitations of digital tools in humanistic research. Its approach to “distant reading” — surfacing quantitative patterns across large text corpora — parallels our own use of the PEN America dataset to identify patterns across hundreds of book challenge incidents. Like Voyant, our project must grapple with the gap between what quantitative patterns reveal and what they cannot capture about meaning, context, and human experience.
This article describes a digital humanities project that attempts to aggregate and standardize data on book bans in U.S. prisons, where censorship practices are opaque, inconsistently documented, and largely invisible to the public. The author uses participatory action research and interviews with advocacy organizations to identify the gaps in existing data and prototype a community-powered reporting tool. This project is directly relevant to ours because it grapples with the same core challenge we face: how to build a meaningful dataset about censorship when much of the censorship itself goes unreported. The author’s finding that relying on official sources alone is insufficient mirrors our own concern that the PEN America index captures only a fraction of actual book removals in California schools. Her emphasis on transparency, community participation, and acknowledging data gaps informs our approach to ethical data practice.

07 Data & Methods

Data & methods

Every figure on this page is generated from a single, openly published dataset (js/data.js). We document our source, scope, and limitations here so the analysis can be inspected, reused, and reproduced.

Source. All challenge records derive from PEN America’s Index of School Book Bans, which catalogs reported challenges by title, author, district, state, and academic year.

Scope. We include records coded as California public-school challenges affecting elementary (K–5) settings between 2021 and 2025. The unit of analysis is a single reported challenge — an attempt to remove or restrict access to a title.

Limitations. The index records only reported challenges; quiet removals and self-censorship leave no trace (see Ethical Considerations). The figures currently display clearly-labelled illustrative placeholder values pending final data entry; replace them in js/data.js to publish real findings.
